\section{Our Contribution}
\label{sec:contribution}

We mathematically define a family of dependency graphs from \module{Mathlib} and analyze them as a multi-layer network. The three primary layers are the module import graph~$G_{\mathrm{module}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-graph}), the declaration dependency graph~$G_{\mathrm{thm}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:thm-graph}), and the namespace graphs~$G_{\mathrm{ns}}^{(k)}$ (Definition~\ref{def:ns-graph}), together with the module tree~$T$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-tree}). %Each raw graph, however, entangles inherited mathematical structure with human organizational process. 
We then define a suite of graph decompositions (\S\ref{sec:definitions}, Table~\ref{tab:decompositions}): edge-level partitions (statement/proof, explicit/synthesized), mechanism-specific subgraphs (typeclass instances, coercions, structure inheritance, auto-derived edges), module-level projections (visibility, build, import utilization), and node-level attributes (definitional height, tactic usage, universe polymorphism). This formalization treats \module{Mathlib} as a graph-theoretic object in which the inherited/organizational distinction is not merely an interpretive lens but a measurable, decomposable structure. 

These definitions constitute a new \emph{mode of inquiry}. They serve as an invitation to study formalized mathematics not theorem by theorem, but as an interconnected system whose global topology reveals organizational patterns and infrastructure dependencies that no local reading of proofs can expose.

To operationalize this systemic view, we apply a suite of standard network-theoretic metrics to each of the three dependency layers. We compute degree distributions, DAG depth profiles, centrality rankings, community detection, containment analysis, and robustness under node removal. The resulting quantitative characterization serves as a preliminary empirical baseline for the macroscopic structure of formalized mathematics, with actionable implications for library maintenance, AI-driven formalization, and tooling (\S\ref{sec:definitions}; Appendices~\ref{app:module-detail}--\ref{app:ns-detail}).

The dependency networks we formalize are not artifacts of our analysis---they are objective structures that have already shaped engineering decisions in the Lean ecosystem, even without explicit network-theoretic framing.
%
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Unused imports.} Mario Carneiro's \texttt{shake} tool~\cite{baanen2025} warns about imports whose exported declarations are never used by the importing module, and runs as part of Mathlib's CI since February 2024. The $17.5\%$ \emph{transitive} redundancy we measure (\S\ref{sec:transitive-reduction}) is the residual after \texttt{shake}: imports that are transitively implied by other imports but that \texttt{shake} intentionally preserves (e.g., for typeclass instances or notation). A cross-version comparison confirms that \texttt{shake} has kept the mean imports per file stable at ${\sim}3.1$ even as the library nearly doubled in size ($3{,}893$ files in January 2024 $\to$ $7{,}563$ in February 2026).
\item \textbf{Transitive redundancy detection.} The \texttt{\#redundant\_imports} command in the \texttt{importGraph} tool~\cite{importgraph} detects the complementary problem: imports that are logically unnecessary because the target module is already reachable through other import paths.
\item \textbf{Transitive visibility control.} Lean's experimental module system~\cite{lean4modules}, introduced in v4.20.0 (June 2025~\cite{lean4v4200}) with the \texttt{module} keyword available since v4.22.0 (August 2025~\cite{lean4v4220}) and migration support in v4.25.0 (November 2025~\cite{lean4v4250}), distinguishes \texttt{public import} from \texttt{private import}, directly reshaping the transitive visibility structure of $G_{\mathrm{module}}$ captured by our visibility graph $G_{\mathrm{mod}}^{\mathrm{pub}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:visibility-graph}). Our snapshot captures a transitional state: $98.8\%$ of imports ($23{,}462$ of $23{,}749$) are still \texttt{public}, with only $287$ \texttt{private} imports adopted so far---the June 2025 snapshot shows zero \texttt{public}/\texttt{private} distinction, confirming that the migration is in its earliest phase.
\item \textbf{Typeclass synthesis bounds.} Lean's typeclass caching and \texttt{synthInstanceMaxHeartbeats} limit respond to the synthesis complexity encoded in $G_{\mathrm{tc}}$ ($1{,}817$ classes, $29{,}952$ instance edges; Definition~\ref{def:tc-graph}).
\item \textbf{Parallel compilation.} \texttt{lake}'s parallel build scheduler exploits the DAG structure of $G_{\mathrm{build}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:build-graph}) to minimize compilation time along the critical path.
\end{itemize}
%
\noindent These engineering measures already operate on the network structures we formalize---the macroscopic object influences local decisions even when it is not explicitly named. Our primary analysis uses a single snapshot (February 2026); a preliminary cross-version comparison at four time points (January 2024, June 2024, June 2025, February 2026) confirms that key module-level invariants (mean imports per file, import growth rate) are stable under \texttt{shake}'s continuous operation, while the module system's effect on $G_{\mathrm{mod}}^{\mathrm{pub}}$ is just beginning. Full temporal network analysis is deferred to future work (\S\ref{sec:temporal-graphs}).

A systematic network analysis, however, reveals the full landscape of structural patterns that no local engineering perspective can see. The three layers are not scaled versions of one another but represent distinct organizational regimes. At the module level: containment decays from $97.5\%$ to $8.8\%$ at depth~$4$ (\S\ref{sec:module-containment}); import utilization is remarkably low (median $1.6\%$; Definition~\ref{def:import-util}). At the declaration level: the statement/proof decomposition (Definition~\ref{def:stmt-proof-dep}) shows that $43.9\%$ of dependencies are proof-only, driven by proof strategy rather than assertion ($8.1\%$ statement-only, $48.0\%$ mixed); fully $74.2\%$ of edges are elaborator-inserted (Definition~\ref{def:synth-partition}); the coercion graph $G_{\mathrm{coe}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:coercion-graph}) has diameter~$4$; the structure inheritance graph $G_{\mathrm{ext}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:extends-graph}) records $1{,}535$ \texttt{extends} edges; auto-derived instances (Definition~\ref{def:deriving-edges}) contribute a purely tool-generated fragment; definitional height $\delta(d)$ (Definition~\ref{def:def-height}) reaches max~$60$ (median~$7$), measuring kernel-level fragility invisible in $G_{\mathrm{thm}}$; and tactic usage (Definition~\ref{def:tactic-usage}) concentrates on \texttt{rw} ($16.9\%$), \texttt{simp} ($11.2\%$), \texttt{exact} ($10.4\%$) across $79{,}910$ proofs. The hub structure separates into a \emph{language infrastructure} layer (numeral coercions, equality constructors) and a \emph{mathematical infrastructure} layer (\decl{CategoryTheory.Category}, \decl{Real}, \decl{TopologicalSpace}; \S\ref{sec:thm-types}). Of all theorems, $44.8\%$ are never cited (\S\ref{sec:thm-leaves}). Cross-namespace edges account for $50.9\%$ of declaration-level dependencies (\S\ref{sec:thm-cross-namespace}), far exceeding the $38.6\%$ at the module level (\S\ref{sec:cross-namespace}). Across layers: NMI\,$=$\,$0.71$ (module--namespace) but only $0.34$ (module--declaration; \S\ref{sec:sa-community}); the module layer compresses ${\sim}40\times$ in nodes and ${\sim}360\times$ in edges (\S\ref{sec:module-declaration}); depth profiles are $153$/$84$/$8$ layers (\S\ref{sec:dag-depth}, \S\ref{sec:thm-dag-depth}, \S\ref{sec:ns-dag-depth}); namespace containment decays from $48.9\%$ to ${\sim}14\%$ (\S\ref{sec:containment-decay}).

Many of these patterns have no corresponding engineering response, and this is precisely where explicit network analysis becomes productive. Import utilization (median $1.6\%$) suggests declaration-level imports as a finer-grained alternative to file-level mechanisms. The $74.2\%$ synthesized-edge ratio suggests that AI premise selection tools would benefit from an explicit-only subgraph filtering out elaborator artifacts. Definitional height (max~$60$) constitutes a fragility metric that could be monitored in CI alongside existing linters. The $44.8\%$ leaf-theorem rate quantifies the library's API surface; tracking its evolution across versions would measure whether new contributions extend the library's reach or inflate its interior. Other patterns---the $50.9\%$ cross-namespace rate, the hub centrality of \decl{CategoryTheory.Category} and \decl{Real}---are natural signals of mathematical structure, not deficiencies to be engineered away. Distinguishing actionable engineering targets from inherent mathematical structure is itself a contribution of network analysis. As de Moura~\cite{demoura2026proofassistants} observes, Lean's users are simultaneously its developers; in such a decentralized process, macroscopic network diagnostics provide guidance that no single contributor's local view can supply. Comparing metrics before and after an intervention---a linter deployment, a module refactoring, a language-level change---directly measures its structural effect on the library as a whole. \xinze{More broadly, our work establishes a correspondence between local engineering actions and macroscopic network indicators: each past intervention (e.g., \texttt{shake}, the module system) can be re-read as a targeted modification of a specific network property. As these correspondences accumulate, the network analysis itself becomes a forward-looking instrument---new engineering decisions can be informed by the current network diagnostics rather than re-discovered from local symptoms. Information shapes practice; practice, in turn, enriches the information available for the next decision.}
