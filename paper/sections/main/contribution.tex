\section{Our Contribution}
\label{sec:contribution}

We mathematically define a family of dependency graphs from \module{Mathlib} and analyze them as a multi-layer network. The three primary layers are the module import graph~$G_{\mathrm{module}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-graph}), the declaration dependency graph~$G_{\mathrm{thm}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:thm-graph}), and the namespace graphs~$G_{\mathrm{ns}}^{(k)}$ (Definition~\ref{def:ns-graph}), together with the module tree~$T$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-tree}). %Each raw graph, however, entangles inherited mathematical structure with human organizational process.
We then define a suite of graph decompositions (Appendix~\ref{sec:graph-definitions}, Table~\ref{tab:decompositions}): edge-level partitions (statement/proof, explicit/synthesized), mechanism-specific subgraphs (typeclass instances, coercions, structure inheritance, auto-derived edges), module-level projections (visibility, build, import utilization), and node-level attributes (definitional height, tactic usage, universe polymorphism). This formalization treats \module{Mathlib} as a graph-theoretic object in which the inherited/organizational distinction is not merely an interpretive lens but a measurable, decomposable structure.

These definitions constitute a new \emph{mode of inquiry}. They serve as an invitation to study formalized mathematics not theorem by theorem, but as an interconnected system whose global topology reveals organizational patterns and infrastructure dependencies that no local reading of proofs can expose.

To operationalize this systemic view, we apply a suite of standard network-theoretic metrics to each of the three dependency layers. We compute degree distributions, DAG depth profiles, centrality rankings, community detection, containment analysis, and robustness under node removal. The resulting quantitative characterization serves as a preliminary empirical baseline for the macroscopic structure of formalized mathematics, with actionable implications for library maintenance, AI-driven formalization, and tooling (Appendix~\ref{sec:graph-definitions}; Appendices~\ref{app:module-detail}--\ref{app:ns-detail}).

The dependency networks we formalize pre-exist our definitions---they are objective structures whose information content has already been acted upon by the Lean ecosystem, albeit without explicit network-theoretic framing. What our formalization provides is not the network itself but the \emph{explicit representation} that makes these correspondences visible, measurable, and comparable. Our empirical findings organize into three process-traces---measurable imprints of human organizational practice embedded in the mathematical product---which we summarize below. Where the Lean ecosystem has already responded to a structural pattern, we note the corresponding engineering tool; distinguishing actionable targets from inherent structure is itself a contribution of network analysis.

These data points are drawn from a cross-version comparison at four Mathlib snapshots (\texttt{b763c21}, January 2024; \texttt{13cef76}, June 2024; \texttt{a3cf05e}, June 2025; \texttt{534cf0b}, February 2026), confirming that engineering responses have measurable network-level effects even before the network is formally named. Full temporal network analysis is deferred to future work (\S\ref{sec:temporal-graphs}).

\paragraph{Finding 1: Organizational divergence and cognitive compression.}
The two human organizational systems---file layout and namespace naming---are internally coherent but collectively misaligned with the dependency structure discovered by network analysis. The proof assistant's type system adds a tool-infrastructure layer that has no traditional mathematical counterpart.
\emph{Organizational alignment.}
NMI\,$=$\,$0.71$ between namespaces and modules (human--human agreement) versus $0.34$ between namespaces and dependency-based communities (human--logic divergence; \S\ref{sec:sa-community}). Cross-namespace edges account for $50.9\%$ of declaration-level dependencies (\S\ref{sec:thm-cross-namespace}), far exceeding the $37.1\%$ at the module level (\S\ref{sec:cross-namespace}). Namespace containment decays from $48.9\%$ to ${\sim}14\%$ at depth~$2$ (\S\ref{sec:containment-decay}); module containment decays from $97.5\%$ to $8.8\%$ at depth~$4$ (\S\ref{sec:module-containment}).

\emph{Hub structure.}
The hub structure separates into a \emph{language infrastructure} layer (elaborator-inserted coercions and equality reasoning) and a \emph{mathematical infrastructure} layer (\decl{CategoryTheory.Category}, \decl{Real}, \decl{TopologicalSpace}; \S\ref{sec:thm-types}). Fully $74.2\%$ of edges are elaborator-inserted rather than human-written (the explicit/synthesized partition of Definition~\ref{def:synth-partition}).

\emph{Tool-infrastructure decompositions.}
Lean's typeclass resolution produces the typeclass instance graph~$G_{\mathrm{tc}}$ ($1{,}817$ classes, $29{,}952$ instance edges; Definition~\ref{def:tc-graph}). The coercion graph $G_{\mathrm{coe}}$ (implicit type conversions; Definition~\ref{def:coercion-graph}) has diameter~$4$; the structure inheritance graph $G_{\mathrm{ext}}$ (\texttt{extends} relationships; Definition~\ref{def:extends-graph}) records $1{,}535$ edges; auto-derived instances (Definition~\ref{def:deriving-edges}) contribute a purely tool-generated fragment of $G_{\mathrm{thm}}$.

\emph{Compression.}
The module layer compresses ${\sim}40\times$ in nodes and ${\sim}360\times$ in edges relative to the declaration layer (\S\ref{sec:module-declaration}).

\paragraph{Finding 2: Structural redundancy as development ergonomics.}
The module import graph contains a significant layer of redundancy that is not inefficiency but intentional development infrastructure: developers import for ergonomics (refactoring, extensibility, instance availability), not along minimal dependency paths.
The \texttt{shake} linter~\cite{baanen2025} responds to edge redundancy in $G_{\mathrm{module}}$---and our cross-version data confirms it has kept mean imports per file stable at ${\sim}3.1$ as the library doubled from $3{,}893$ to $7{,}563$ files. The \texttt{\#redundant\_imports} command~\cite{importgraph} detects the $17.5\%$ transitive redundancy ($4{,}122$ edges; \S\ref{sec:transitive-reduction}) that \texttt{shake} intentionally preserves. Import utilization---the fraction of an imported module's declarations actually used by the importer (Definition~\ref{def:import-util})---is remarkably low (median $1.6\%$), suggesting that declaration-level imports would be a more precise alternative. The \texttt{public}/\texttt{private} import distinction~\cite{lean4modules,lean4v4200,lean4v4220,lean4v4250} reshapes the transitive visibility captured by $G_{\mathrm{mod}}^{\mathrm{pub}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:visibility-graph})---our snapshot shows the migration just beginning, with $98.8\%$ of imports still \texttt{public} ($287$ \texttt{private}).

\paragraph{Finding 3: Topological inversion and semantic flattening.}
The three dependency layers exhibit strikingly different topologies: human-organized structure is the deepest while logical structure is the shallowest. Formalization preserves traditional labels but flattens their meaning, and the process of proving---not the content of assertions---drives the majority of the dependency structure.
The depth profiles are $153$/$84$/$8$ layers across the three graphs (\S\ref{sec:dag-depth}, \S\ref{sec:thm-dag-depth}, \S\ref{sec:ns-dag-depth}); \texttt{lake}'s parallel scheduler exploits this DAG structure (Definition~\ref{def:build-graph})---the critical path traverses $161$ modules with a parallelism ratio of $22.4\times$. The statement/proof decomposition---classifying each edge by whether it arises from the statement, the proof, or both (Definition~\ref{def:stmt-proof-dep})---shows that $43.9\%$ of dependencies are proof-only ($8.1\%$ statement-only, $48.0\%$ mixed), meaning proof strategy---not mathematical assertion---drives most of the dependency structure. Of all theorems, $44.8\%$ are never cited by any declaration inside \module{Mathlib} (\S\ref{sec:thm-leaves})---these terminal nodes form the outer boundary of the dependency graph, whose consumers are necessarily external. Definitional height---the longest chain of nested \texttt{unfold} steps to reach primitives (Definition~\ref{def:def-height})---reaches max~$60$ (median~$7$), a fragility metric invisible in the explicit dependency graph that could be monitored in CI. Tactic usage---the per-proof distribution of proof commands (Definition~\ref{def:tactic-usage})---concentrates on \texttt{rw} ($16.9\%$), \texttt{simp} ($11.2\%$), \texttt{exact} ($10.4\%$) across $79{,}910$ proofs, with significant per-namespace variation reflecting distinct proof methodologies. AI premise selection tools would benefit from the explicit-only subgraph (Definition~\ref{def:synth-partition}), which removes elaborator-inserted edges to expose the human-written dependency structure.
