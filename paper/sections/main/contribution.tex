The three primary layers are the module import graph~$G_{\mathrm{module}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-graph}), the declaration dependency graph~$G_{\mathrm{thm}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:thm-graph}), and the namespace graphs~$G_{\mathrm{ns}}^{(k)}$ (Definition~\ref{def:ns-graph}), together with the module tree~$T$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-tree}). %Each raw graph, however, entangles inherited mathematical structure with human organizational process.
We then define a suite of graph decompositions (Appendix~\ref{sec:graph-definitions}, Table~\ref{tab:decompositions}): edge-level partitions (statement/proof, explicit/synthesized), mechanism-specific subgraphs (typeclass instances, coercions, structure inheritance, auto-derived edges), module-level projections (visibility, build, import utilization), and node-level attributes (definitional height, tactic usage, universe polymorphism). This formalization treats \module{Mathlib} as a graph theoretical object in which the distinction between inherited and organizational elements functions not merely as an interpretive lens, but as a measurable and decomposable structure. These definitions serve as an invitation to study formalized mathematics also as an interconnected system whose global topology reveals organizational patterns and infrastructure dependencies that no local reading of proofs can expose.

The dependency networks we formalize predate our definitions. They constitute objective structures whose information content has already been acted upon by the Lean ecosystem, albeit without explicit network theoretical framing. What our formalization provides is not the network itself, but rather the \emph{explicit representation} that makes these correspondences visible, measurable, and comparable. Our empirical findings organize into three process traces, representing measurable imprints of human organizational practice embedded within the mathematical product, which we summarize below. In cases where the Lean ecosystem has already responded to a structural pattern, we identify the corresponding engineering tool. Distinguishing actionable targets from inherent structure is itself a contribution of network analysis.

Our data points are drawn from a cross-version comparison at four Mathlib snapshots (\texttt{b763c21}, January 2024; \texttt{13cef76}, June 2024; \texttt{a3cf05e}, June 2025; \texttt{534cf0b}, February 2026), confirming that engineering responses have measurable network-level effects even before the network is formally named. \paragraph{Finding 1: Organizational divergence.}
The dual human-designed organizational systems of file layout and namespace naming exhibit strong internal coherence, yet they diverge fundamentally from the logical dependency structure revealed by network analysis. Concurrently, the type system of the proof assistant introduces a layer of tooling infrastructure that lacks any traditional mathematical counterpart.

\emph{Organizational alignment.}
The Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) score reaches $0.71$ between namespaces and modules, reflecting strong agreement among human-organized structures. Conversely, the score drops to $0.34$ between namespaces and dependency-based communities, highlighting a stark divergence between human taxonomy and underlying logic (\S\ref{sec:sa-community}). Cross-namespace edges constitute $50.9\%$ of dependencies at the declaration level (\S\ref{sec:thm-cross-namespace}), substantially exceeding the $37.1\%$ observed at the module level (\S\ref{sec:cross-namespace}). Structural containment also dissipates rapidly across hierarchies: namespace containment drops from $48.9\%$ to roughly $14\%$ by depth~$2$ (\S\ref{sec:containment-decay}), while module containment falls from $97.5\%$ to $8.8\%$ by depth~$4$ (\S\ref{sec:module-containment}).

\emph{Hub structure.}
The network's hub structure naturally bifurcates into two distinct layers: a \emph{language infrastructure} layer that manages coercions and equality reasoning, and a \emph{mathematical infrastructure} layer that grounds core concepts such as \decl{CategoryTheory.Category}, \decl{Real}, and \decl{TopologicalSpace} (\S\ref{sec:thm-types}). This foundational infrastructure is heavily automated; a striking $74.2\%$ of all edges are compiler-generated rather than explicitly authored by humans, as demonstrated by the explicit/synthesized partition (Definition~\ref{def:synth-partition}). \violet{How was this measured?}

\emph{Tool infrastructure decompositions.}
Lean's typeclass resolution mechanism generates the typeclass instance graph~$G_{\mathrm{tc}}$, comprising $1{,}817$ classes and $29{,}952$ instance edges (Definition~\ref{def:tc-graph}). Supporting this ecosystem are several specialized subgraphs. The coercion graph~$G_{\mathrm{coe}}$ maps implicit type conversions and exhibits a diameter of~$4$ (Definition~\ref{def:coercion-graph}), while the structure inheritance graph~$G_{\mathrm{ext}}$ captures $1{,}535$ \texttt{extends} relationships (Definition~\ref{def:extends-graph}). Together with automatically derived instances (Definition~\ref{def:deriving-edges}), these elements constitute a completely tool-generated fragment of the broader mathematical graph~$G_{\mathrm{thm}}$.

The module import graph contains a significant layer of redundancy. Rather than indicating inefficiency, this structure serves as intentional development infrastructure. Developers prioritize ergonomics (such as refactoring, extensibility, and instance availability) over minimal dependency paths. The \texttt{shake} linter~\cite{baanen2025} manages edge redundancy within $G_{\mathrm{module}}$. Data across versions confirms that this tool has kept the mean imports per file stable at ${\sim}3.1$, even as the library doubled from $3{,}893$ to $7{,}563$ files. At the same time, the \texttt{\#redundant\_imports} command~\cite{importgraph} detects the $17.5\%$ transitive redundancy ($4{,}122$ edges; \S\ref{sec:transitive-reduction}) that \texttt{shake} intentionally preserves. Import utilization (the fraction of an imported module's declarations actually used by the importer, Definition~\ref{def:import-util}) is remarkably low at a median of $1.6\%$. This suggests that imports at the declaration level would offer a more precise alternative. Finally, the \texttt{public}/\texttt{private} import distinction~\cite{lean4modules,lean4v4200,lean4v4220,lean4v4250} reshapes the transitive visibility captured by $G_{\mathrm{mod}}^{\mathrm{pub}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:visibility-graph}). Our snapshot reveals that this migration is just beginning, since $98.8\%$ of imports are still \texttt{public} ($287$ \texttt{private}).

\paragraph{Finding 3: Semantic flattening.}
The three dependency layers exhibit strikingly different topologies, where human-organized structures are significantly deeper than logical ones. While formalization preserves traditional mathematical labels, it flattens their semantic meaning; consequently, the dependency structure is driven primarily by the process of proving rather than the content of the assertions. The depth profiles span $153$, $84$, and $8$ layers across the three respective graphs (\S\ref{sec:dag-depth}, \S\ref{sec:thm-dag-depth}, \S\ref{sec:ns-dag-depth}). The parallel scheduler in \texttt{lake} exploits this directed acyclic graph structure (Definition~\ref{def:build-graph}), managing a critical path that traverses $161$ modules with a parallelism ratio of $22.4\times$. Edge classification (Definition~\ref{def:stmt-proof-dep}) further emphasizes the dominance of proofs: $43.9\%$ of dependencies arise exclusively from proofs, compared to only $8.1\%$ from statements and $48.0\%$ from both. At the network's periphery, $44.8\%$ of all theorems act as terminal nodes that are never cited within \module{Mathlib} (\S\ref{sec:thm-leaves}), indicating that their consumers are inherently external. Beneath this visible topology lies definitional height (Definition~\ref{def:def-height}), which tracks the longest chain of nested \texttt{unfold} steps required to reach primitives. Peaking at $60$ steps with a median of $7$, this represents a hidden fragility metric highly suitable for continuous integration monitoring. At the implementation level, tactic usage (Definition~\ref{def:tactic-usage}) across $79{,}910$ proofs concentrates on \texttt{rw} ($16.9\%$), \texttt{simp} ($11.2\%$), and \texttt{exact} ($10.4\%$), displaying notable variations across namespaces that reflect distinct methodologies. Finally, to better isolate the human-authored dependency structure for downstream tasks like AI premise selection, we recommend using the explicit subgraph (Definition~\ref{def:synth-partition}), which filters out compiler-generated edges.
