\section{Our Contribution}
\label{sec:contribution}

We mathematically define a family of dependency graphs from \module{Mathlib} and analyze them as a multi-layer network. The three primary layers are the module import graph~$G_{\mathrm{module}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-graph}), the declaration dependency graph~$G_{\mathrm{thm}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:thm-graph}), and the namespace graphs~$G_{\mathrm{ns}}^{(k)}$ (Definition~\ref{def:ns-graph}), together with the module tree~$T$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-tree}). %Each raw graph, however, entangles inherited mathematical structure with human organizational process. 
We then define a suite of graph decompositions (\S\ref{sec:definitions}, Table~\ref{tab:decompositions}): edge-level partitions (statement/proof, explicit/synthesized), mechanism-specific subgraphs (typeclass instances, coercions, structure inheritance, auto-derived edges), module-level projections (visibility, build, import utilization), and node-level attributes (definitional height, tactic usage, universe polymorphism). This formalization treats \module{Mathlib} as a graph-theoretic object in which the inherited/organizational distinction is not merely an interpretive lens but a measurable, decomposable structure. 

These definitions constitute a new \emph{mode of inquiry}. They serve as an invitation to study formalized mathematics not theorem by theorem, but as an interconnected system whose global topology reveals organizational patterns and infrastructure dependencies that no local reading of proofs can expose.

To operationalize this systemic view, we apply a suite of standard network-theoretic metrics to each of the three dependency layers. We compute degree distributions, DAG depth profiles, centrality rankings, community detection, containment analysis, and robustness under node removal. The resulting quantitative characterization serves as a preliminary empirical baseline for the macroscopic structure of formalized mathematics, with actionable implications for library maintenance, AI-driven formalization, and tooling (\S\ref{sec:definitions}; Appendices~\ref{app:module-detail}--\ref{app:ns-detail}).

The dependency networks we formalize pre-exist our definitions---they are objective structures whose information content has already been acted upon by the Lean ecosystem, albeit without explicit network-theoretic framing. Many engineering decisions that appear to arise from local observation are, in fact, implicit responses to macroscopic network properties: the \texttt{shake} linter~\cite{baanen2025} responds to edge redundancy in $G_{\mathrm{module}}$---and our cross-version data confirms it has kept mean imports per file stable at ${\sim}3.1$ as the library doubled from $3{,}893$ to $7{,}563$ files; the \texttt{\#redundant\_imports} command~\cite{importgraph} detects the $17.5\%$ transitive redundancy ($4{,}122$ edges; \S\ref{sec:transitive-reduction}) that \texttt{shake} intentionally preserves; the \texttt{public}/\texttt{private} import distinction~\cite{lean4modules,lean4v4200,lean4v4220,lean4v4250} reshapes the transitive visibility captured by $G_{\mathrm{mod}}^{\mathrm{pub}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:visibility-graph})---our snapshot shows the migration just beginning, with $98.8\%$ of imports still \texttt{public} ($287$ \texttt{private}); Lean's typeclass caching bounds the synthesis complexity encoded in $G_{\mathrm{tc}}$ ($1{,}817$ classes, $29{,}952$ instance edges, $\sigma = 74.2\%$ synthesized; Definition~\ref{def:tc-graph}); and \texttt{lake}'s parallel scheduler exploits the DAG structure of $G_{\mathrm{build}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:build-graph})---the critical path traverses $161$ modules with a parallelism ratio of $22.4\times$. What our formalization provides is not the network itself but the \emph{explicit representation} that makes these correspondences visible, measurable, and comparable.

These data points are drawn from a cross-version comparison at four Mathlib snapshots (\texttt{b763c21}, January 2024; \texttt{13cef76}, June 2024; \texttt{a3cf05e}, June 2025; \texttt{534cf0b}, February 2026), confirming that engineering responses have measurable network-level effects even before the network is formally named. Full temporal network analysis is deferred to future work (\S\ref{sec:temporal-graphs}).

At the same time, our analysis reveals structural patterns that have no corresponding engineering response, and poses new questions. At the module level, import utilization is remarkably low (median $1.6\%$; Definition~\ref{def:import-util}), suggesting that declaration-level imports would be a more precise alternative; module containment decays from $97.5\%$ to $8.8\%$ at depth~$4$ (\S\ref{sec:module-containment}). At the declaration level, the statement/proof decomposition (Definition~\ref{def:stmt-proof-dep}) shows that $43.9\%$ of dependencies are proof-only ($8.1\%$ statement-only, $48.0\%$ mixed), meaning proof strategy---not mathematical assertion---drives most of the dependency structure. Fully $74.2\%$ of edges are elaborator-inserted (Definition~\ref{def:synth-partition}), suggesting that AI premise selection tools would benefit from an explicit-only subgraph. The coercion graph $G_{\mathrm{coe}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:coercion-graph}) has diameter~$4$; the structure inheritance graph $G_{\mathrm{ext}}$ (Definition~\ref{def:extends-graph}) records $1{,}535$ \texttt{extends} edges; auto-derived instances (Definition~\ref{def:deriving-edges}) contribute a purely tool-generated fragment of $G_{\mathrm{thm}}$. Definitional height (Definition~\ref{def:def-height}) reaches max~$60$ (median~$7$), a fragility metric invisible in the explicit dependency graph that could be monitored in CI. Tactic usage (Definition~\ref{def:tactic-usage}) concentrates on \texttt{rw} ($16.9\%$), \texttt{simp} ($11.2\%$), \texttt{exact} ($10.4\%$) across $79{,}910$ proofs. Of all theorems, $44.8\%$ are never cited (\S\ref{sec:thm-leaves}), quantifying the library's API surface.

Not all patterns call for engineering intervention. Cross-namespace edges account for $50.9\%$ of declaration-level dependencies (\S\ref{sec:thm-cross-namespace}), far exceeding the $38.6\%$ at the module level (\S\ref{sec:cross-namespace}); the hub structure separates into a \emph{language infrastructure} layer and a \emph{mathematical infrastructure} layer (\decl{CategoryTheory.Category}, \decl{Real}, \decl{TopologicalSpace}; \S\ref{sec:thm-types}); the depth profiles are $153$/$84$/$8$ layers across the three graphs (\S\ref{sec:dag-depth}, \S\ref{sec:thm-dag-depth}, \S\ref{sec:ns-dag-depth}); NMI\,$=$\,$0.71$ (module--namespace) vs.\ $0.34$ (module--declaration; \S\ref{sec:sa-community}); and the module layer compresses ${\sim}40\times$ in nodes and ${\sim}360\times$ in edges relative to the declaration layer (\S\ref{sec:module-declaration}). Namespace containment decays from $48.9\%$ to ${\sim}14\%$ at depth~$2$ (\S\ref{sec:containment-decay}). These are natural signatures of the mathematical structure itself, not deficiencies to be engineered away. Distinguishing actionable targets from inherent structure is itself a contribution of network analysis.

Each past intervention can be re-read as a targeted modification of a network property; as these correspondences accumulate, the network analysis becomes a forward-looking instrument. Information shapes practice; practice enriches the information available for the next decision.
