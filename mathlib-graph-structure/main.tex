\begin{abstract}
Formal mathematics libraries are strictly deterministic artifacts: every dependency is machine-verified, and the compiled artifact contains no ambiguity. Yet these libraries are built by humans through an uncertain process of exploration, organization, and judgment. We analyze the network structure of Mathlib---the largest unified library of formalized mathematics---to demonstrate that the deterministic product preserves measurable traces of the cognitive process that created it. Our analysis reveals three specific signatures of this human influence: a deep ``funnel'' topology (153 layers), a 17.5\% rate of transitively redundant imports, and a near-zero module cohesion (mean 0.025). These metrics indicate that developers align dependencies with human-readable ``cognitive containers'' rather than minimal logical requirements. We conclude that formalization does not dissolve human organization; instead, the ``cognitive habits'' of contributors are encoded in the dependency topology, structurally reshaping the logical product.
\end{abstract}
