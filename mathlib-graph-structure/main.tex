\begin{abstract}
We study the dependency structure of \module{Mathlib}, the largest unified library of formalized mathematics, comprising $7{,}563$ modules and $308{,}129$ declarations connected by $8.4$ million dependency edges. We construct and analyze dependency graphs at three granularities---modules (source files), declarations (theorems, definitions, and axioms), and namespaces (hierarchical name prefixes)---and measure how these levels interact.

We find that the two human organizational systems---file structure and namespace naming---are highly consistent with each other (NMI $= 0.71$), but both diverge significantly from the logical dependency structure: module cohesion averages $0.107$, meaning only $10.7\%$ of a module's dependency traffic stays internal, and Louvain community detection recovers mathematical disciplines with limited alignment to human-defined namespaces (NMI $= 0.34$). The declaration-level graph exhibits a two-layer hub structure: a language infrastructure layer determined by Lean's type system (equality reasoning, numeral coercions) and a mathematical infrastructure layer determined by mathematical content (categories, real numbers, modules). Of all cross-file dependencies at the declaration level, $92.2\%$ are indirect---they do not correspond to any explicit import statement. A containment decay analysis across namespace depths shows that human cognitive classification retains organizing power only at the coarsest granularity ($48.9\%$ at the top-level directory) and drops to ${\sim}13\%$ at finer levels.

These structural metrics provide quantitative baselines for library maintenance, language design evaluation, and AI-assisted formalization.
\end{abstract}
