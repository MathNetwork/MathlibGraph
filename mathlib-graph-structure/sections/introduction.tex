\section{Introduction}

Mathematical formalization is undergoing a transformation from artisanal craft to industrial-scale engineering. Mathlib---the largest unified mathematical library for the Lean~4 proof assistant~\cite{mathlib2020,lean4}---comprises over $7{,}500$ modules and hundreds of thousands of formally verified theorems, spanning from foundational logic to frontier number theory~\cite{mathlib_community}. It is no longer an experimental project but a living infrastructure, collaboratively maintained by hundreds of contributors.

Yet formalization, in solving one problem, creates another. It turns every step of mathematical reasoning into a verifiable, deterministic product: the type checker delivers a yes-or-no verdict, with no room for ambiguity. The process that produces these deterministic products, however---proof search, conceptual exploration, cognitive navigation, community collaboration---is inherently uncertain, tentative, and reliant on intuition. Formalization makes the product precise, but it does not eliminate the uncertainty of the process; rather, it encodes traces of that process into the structure of the product in new and measurable ways.

The central claim of this paper is that the network structure of a formal mathematical library records not only the logical dependencies of mathematics, but also the cognitive habits and organizational traces of mathematical practice. By analyzing these network structures, we can read back, from the completed deterministic product, the traces of the uncertain process that produced it.

We take Mathlib as our object of study and develop our analysis at three progressively finer levels of granularity:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Module-level graph structures (\S3).}
The coarsest granularity. Each module (source file) is a node; \texttt{import} statements form edges. We analyze two graphs simultaneously---the namespace tree~$T$, which encodes the human cognitive classification of mathematics, and the import graph~$G_{\mathrm{import}}$, which encodes the compiler-verified logical dependencies---together with the systematic divergence between them. This granularity captures the \emph{architecture} of the library: its overall shape, organizational principles, and structural vulnerabilities.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Theorem--premise dependency graph (\S4).}
A finer granularity. Each theorem or definition is a node; the premises invoked in a proof form edges. This level penetrates into the mathematical content itself---no longer dependencies between files, but logical relations between theorems. It measures the \emph{reasoning structure} of mathematics: which theorems serve as core lemmas, which are isolated special cases, and how knowledge flows across theories.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Module--declaration interaction (\S5).}
The first two levels of analysis treat modules and declarations as separate objects. In \S5 we overlay them: each module becomes a \emph{region} in the theorem--premise graph, and we measure how well the human-drawn module boundaries align with the logical dependency structure. Module cohesion---the proportion of a module's dependency traffic that stays internal---quantifies this alignment. This cross-level analysis makes the central tension concrete: module boundaries are a human decision (process), while declaration-level dependencies are determined by logical necessity (product).

\medskip
Together, the three levels form a progression from coarse architecture (\S3) through fine-grained logical structure (\S4) to the tension between human organization and mathematical necessity (\S5).

\section{Previous works}

Several natural graph structures arise from Mathlib. At the coarsest level, the \emph{module import graph} is a directed acyclic graph whose nodes are \texttt{.lean} files and whose edges represent \texttt{import} statements; an official tool for generating this graph ships with the library~\cite{importgraph}.
At a finer level, the \emph{theorem--premise dependency graph} connects each theorem to the lemmas and definitions invoked in its proof. Overlaying these two levels---treating each module as a region in the theorem--premise graph---yields a \emph{module--declaration interaction} analysis that measures how well human-imposed file boundaries align with logical dependency structure. These three levels of analysis encode complementary aspects of the organization of formal mathematical knowledge.

The graph structure of Mathlib has been exploited in machine learning, particularly for premise selection. Yang et al.~\cite{leandojo} introduced the LeanDojo benchmark, extracting proof traces from Mathlib that implicitly define a dependency graph. Bemberek et al.~\cite{bemberek2025} constructed a heterogeneous dependency graph from this benchmark and applied relational graph convolutional networks for premise selection, achieving a 25\% improvement over text-only baselines. Lu et al.~\cite{leanfinder} built a directed acyclic graph of Lean~4 statements with dependency edges to support semantic search. On the knowledge-representation side, the EpiK Protocol~\cite{epik2024} constructed a knowledge graph from Lean~4 and Mathlib, applying embedding models for link prediction. Uskuplu and Moss~\cite{knowtex2025} argued that dependency graphs should become a standard feature of mathematical writing. In a related but distinct setting, Barbosa et al.~\cite{barbosa2013} studied the network structure of three online mathematical libraries---Wikipedia (restricted to mathematics), MathWorld, and DLMF---finding large strongly connected components, the absence of clear power laws, and the effectiveness of stress centrality for navigating mathematical content.
