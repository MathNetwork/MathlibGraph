\section{Introduction}

Mathlib is the principal mathematical library for the Lean~4 proof assistant, comprising a large and continuously growing corpus of formalized mathematics~\cite{mathlib2020,lean4}. Developed collaboratively by a global community of contributors, it encodes definitions, lemmas, and theorems spanning algebra, analysis, topology, combinatorics, number theory, and many other areas into machine-checkable proofs~\cite{mathlib_community}. With nearly two million lines of formalized mathematics, Mathlib has become one of the most extensive libraries available in any proof assistant.

Several natural graph structures arise from Mathlib. At the \textcolor{red}{coarsest level}, the \emph{module import graph} is a directed acyclic graph whose nodes are \texttt{.lean} files and whose edges represent \texttt{import} statements; an official tool for generating this graph ships with the library~\cite{importgraph}. 
At a \textcolor{red}{finer level}, the \emph{theorem--premise dependency graph} connects each theorem to the lemmas and definitions invoked in its proof. A \textcolor{red}{third construction}, the \emph{premise co-occurrence graph}, places an undirected edge between two premises whenever they appear together in sufficiently many proofs. These graphs encode complementary aspects of the organization of formal mathematical knowledge.

The graph structure of Mathlib has been exploited in machine learning, particularly for premise selection. Yang et al.~\cite{leandojo} introduced the LeanDojo benchmark, extracting proof traces from Mathlib that implicitly define a dependency graph. Bemberek et al.~\cite{bemberek2025} constructed a heterogeneous dependency graph from this benchmark and applied relational graph convolutional networks for premise selection, achieving a 25\% improvement over text-only baselines. Lu et al.~\cite{leanfinder} built a directed acyclic graph of Lean~4 statements with dependency edges to support semantic search. On the knowledge-representation side, the EpiK Protocol~\cite{epik2024} constructed a knowledge graph from Lean~4 and Mathlib, applying embedding models for link prediction. Uskuplu and Moss~\cite{knowtex2025} argued that dependency graphs should become a standard feature of mathematical writing. In a related but distinct setting, Barbosa et al.~\cite{barbosa2013} studied the network structure of three online mathematical libraries---Wikipedia (restricted to mathematics), MathWorld, and DLMF---finding large strongly connected components, the absence of clear power laws, and the effectiveness of stress centrality for navigating mathematical content.
